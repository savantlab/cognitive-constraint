## Executive Summary

Cognitive Constraint Foundation addresses the replication crisis in psychology by publishing peer-reviewed invalidations of flawed psychological models. We operate an innovative open-access journal that pays researchers for rigorous peer review and pays authors for validated publications.

Our model corrects three fundamental problems in academic publishing:
1. **Unpaid labor** - Peer reviewers work for free, reducing quality and speed
2. **Gatekeeping** - Anonymous review protects established theories from valid criticism
3. **No accountability** - Failed replications and invalid models persist for decades

**Key Innovations:**
- Pay reviewers $500-5,000 per review (quality work, fast turnaround)
- Pay authors $5,000-100,000 per accepted paper (incentivize paradigm-shifting research)
- Public, signed reviews (accountability and transparency)
- Reproducibility required (all code, data, proofs public)
- Institutional membership model (elite universities support the foundation)

**Target Market:**
- 8 premier research universities (Year 1-2): $4-8M revenue
- 50 research institutions (Year 3-4): $20-50M revenue  
- 100-200 institutions (Year 5+): $50-150M revenue

**Initial Focus:**
Three major invalidations with massive downstream impact:
1. Mental rotation models in spatial cognition
2. Cognitive complexity theories in developmental psychology
3. IQ testing psychometric foundations

**5-Year Goal:** Become the accreditation standard for methodologically rigorous psychology and cognitive science research.

## Problem Statement

### The Replication Crisis

Psychology and cognitive science face a methodological crisis:
- 60-70% of published findings fail to replicate
- Statistical methods are frequently misapplied
- Models persist despite mathematical errors
- Institutional inertia prevents correction

**Downstream consequences:**
- Clinical trials fail due to invalid cognitive endpoints ($10B+ wasted annually)
- Educational policies based on disproven theories (millions of students affected)
- Legal decisions use invalidated assessments (death penalty, disability claims)
- AI systems trained on pseudoscientific data

### Why Current Journals Fail

**Traditional peer review:**
- Unpaid reviewers provide minimal effort
- Anonymous reviews lack accountability
- Gatekeeping protects established researchers
- 6-18 month timelines delay corrections
- No incentive to publish invalidations (career risk)

**Result:** Invalid models remain in textbooks, curricula, and practice for decades.

## Solution: Paid, Transparent, Rigorous Validation

### Journal Model

**Open submission with high standards:**
- Anyone can submit invalidations of psychological models
- No submission fees (barrier-free)
- Requires: Mathematical proofs, reproducible code/data, clear implications

**Paid peer review:**
- $500-5,000 per review (based on complexity)
- Public, signed reviews (full accountability)
- Payment regardless of approve/reject decision
- 2-4 week turnaround (reviewers prioritize paid work)

**Author payments:**
- $5,000-100,000 per accepted paper (based on scope and impact)
- Immediate payment upon publication
- Additional impact bonuses tied to adoption, citations, policy changes

**Post-publication validation:**
- Community can submit replications (paid bounties)
- Validation scores track reproducibility
- Papers can be challenged with refutation bounties

### Institutional Membership Program

Universities subscribe at tiered levels:

**Tier 1: Observer** ($25,000/year)
- Faculty can read all papers
- Cannot cite in publications
- Cannot submit or review

**Tier 2: Participant** ($100,000-250,000/year)
- Faculty can cite papers
- Eligible for paid peer review
- Cannot submit papers
- Requires demonstrated curriculum reforms

**Tier 3: Publisher** ($500,000-2,000,000/year)
- Full access: cite, review, submit papers
- Priority review for submissions
- Co-branded research initiatives
- Curriculum consulting included
- Early access to invalidations

**Qualification for Tier 3:**
- Evidence of curriculum changes adopting corrected models
- Public commitment to methodological standards
- Removal of invalidated theories from syllabi

**Initial invitations (Year 1-2):**
- Caltech, Princeton, Yale, U Washington, UC Berkeley, Johns Hopkins, UCLA, Five Colleges Consortium

**Expansion (Year 3+):**
- Selective acceptance of additional institutions
- Requires proof of internal reforms
- Creates competitive pressure for methodological rigor

## Governance & Editorial Independence

### Board Structure

**Board of Directors (7 members):**
- Founder (you) - Board Chair
- 3 scientific advisors (senior methodologists, different institutions)
- 2 institutional representatives (from subscribing universities, rotating)
- 1 public interest representative (ethicist, policy expert)

**Editorial Board (10-20 members):**
- Editor-in-Chief (you initially, can delegate)
- Associate Editors by domain (cognitive, clinical, social, methods)
- Independent from Board of Directors
- Cannot be overruled by donors or subscribers

**Conflict of Interest Policies:**
- Editors recuse from papers with personal/institutional conflicts
- Subscriber institutions don't get preferential treatment in review
- Reviewer-author relationships disclosed publicly
- Financial relationships transparent

### Editorial Standards

**Publication criteria:**
1. Mathematical soundness (proofs verified)
2. Computational reproducibility (code runs, results match)
3. Clear implications (field impact documented)
4. Intellectual honesty (alternative explanations considered)

**NOT criteria:**
- Political palatability
- Protecting established theories
- Subscriber preferences
- Citation count or prestige of contradicted work

**Independence charter:**
- Editorial decisions cannot be influenced by funding
- Board cannot override editor on scientific grounds
- Whistleblower protection for authors facing retaliation

## Risk Mitigation

### Legal Risks

**Challenge: Defamation claims from researchers whose work is invalidated**

**Mitigation:**
- Rigorous peer review (defensible scientific basis)
- Public review process (transparency)
- Legal defense fund ($1-2M reserved)
- D&O insurance for board/editors
- First Amendment protections (scientific speech)

**Challenge: Copyright enforcement (citation restrictions)**

**Mitigation:**
- Clear license terms (upfront)
- Partnerships with major publishers (enforcement agreements)
- Selective enforcement (target institutions, not individuals)
- Legal team for DMCA/cease-and-desist

### Reputational Risks

**Challenge: Dismissed as "predatory journal"**

**Mitigation:**
- Elite institutional subscribers (instant credibility)
- Pay authors AFTER acceptance, not before submission
- Higher standards than traditional journals (math + reproducibility)
- Public, signed reviews (more accountable than anonymous)
- Heavyweight editorial board

**Challenge: "Paying for papers = buying results"**

**Mitigation:**
- Payment same whether approved or rejected (reviewers)
- Payment only if paper passes rigorous standards (authors)
- Public review prevents rubber-stamping
- Post-publication challenges with refutation bounties

### Financial Risks

**Challenge: Subscriptions don't materialize**

**Mitigation:**
- Start with grants (NSF, foundations) - less dependent on subscriptions Year 1
- Prove model with 2-3 early adopter schools before scaling
- IQ invalidation creates necessity (schools must subscribe)

**Challenge: Operating deficit**

**Mitigation:**
- Build reserves in Year 2-3 (surpluses from subscriptions)
- Endowment campaign (Year 3-5, target $20-50M)
- Company provides platform support at cost
- Flexible cost structure (can reduce paper volume if needed)
